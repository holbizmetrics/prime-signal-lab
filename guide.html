Guide

What this lab investigated, what it observed, what didn't work, and what's open.

The question

RSA's security rests on the assumption that, given N = p·q, recovering p and q is hard. This lab investigates a narrower form of that question:

Can we extract factor information from N = p·q by analyzing the carries that occur during the multiplication itself?

Carries are the bit-level "remainders" that propagate when you multiply two binary numbers. The intuition behind this lab: carry patterns depend on both operands and both get baked into N. If the carries leave a detectable fingerprint, that fingerprint might leak factor information.

This is not an end-to-end factoring attack. It's a structural investigation: where does factor information live in N, and how much of it survives multiplication?

What we observed — top findings

Each finding below is tagged for verifiability:

  • live reproducible by clicking the linked tool with documented inputs
  • historical documented observation from an earlier experiment; the tool retains the prose but doesn't recompute the exact number on click
  • regime mismatch the headline is on a different experiment than what the tool currently runs
  • tbd-verify awaiting per-tool verification before final labeling

1. Carries leak factor information — measurable through multiple lenses

regime mismatch The widely-quoted r = 0.86 headline (single-feature correlation: maxOneRun → carryChainLength) is a documented historical observation. The current carry-reconstruction-1 tool runs a different, more comprehensive experiment: multi-feature logistic regression predicting the full carry sequence, reporting ~89% bit accuracy with 40% perfect-sequence reconstruction on 10-bit semiprimes (sample = 200). The metrics aren't comparable — different feature counts, different target shape, different reporting axis. Honest interpretation: the signal is robust enough that it survives both experiment shapes; the specific r = 0.86 number is not what the tool measures now.

2. Signals fuse — orthogonal ones compound

live A single carry-extraction signal can reach ~92% accuracy on bit-prediction; combining multiple orthogonal signals (windowed, modular, local, global feature families) pushes ensemble accuracy slightly higher in the live fusion-analysis output (~92.4%). This is the substantive claim: signals are not redundant — different feature families capture different parts of the carry pattern.

3. Bit-level recoverability scales (10-bit → 28-bit, on the experiments tested)

historical The "Research Findings" tab of carry-research-lab documents ~92% / 91% / 92% bit accuracy across 10-bit / 20-bit / 28-bit factors — i.e., the signal does not collapse at slightly larger N. These findings live as prose in the Research Findings tab; the live interactive tabs (Single Trace, Reconstruction, Scaling, etc.) recompute related metrics on click but the headline numbers themselves are from a historical run whose exact regime isn't documented in the current repo. To verify: open the tool, click through the interactive tabs.

4. Information is transformed, not destroyed

tbd-verify The framing surfaced by carry-transformation-analysis: carries don't erase factor information — they encode it into a form that's harder to read. The tool offers recoverability tests; the headline framing is a research thesis rather than a single number to verify.

Open questions

  1. Does the signal scale to RSA-realistic N? The verified work covers ≤ 28-bit factors. RSA-2048 uses ~1024-bit factors. The 92% recoverability at 10/20/28 bits is not evidence that the signal survives — that's a separate experimental program.
  2. Is there a non-trivial theoretical upper bound on what carry-based signals can leak? Information-theoretic ceiling analysis (in carry-reconstruction-1) computes a per-run entropy figure but does not establish a regime-independent bound.
  3. What's the connection to known partial-information attacks? Coppersmith's small-roots, Boneh-Durfee on small-d, May's partial-key-exposure work — these all attack RSA under specific partial-information assumptions. The carry-leakage findings here haven't been formally related to those literatures.
  4. Does the r → 1.0 = factoring solved framing hold up? The framing in carry-reconstruction-1 claims that perfect carry reconstruction implies factoring via onesFromPP = N + 2·carryOut − carryIn → polynomial factoring → p, q. This is presented as an architectural argument, not a proof. Whether the path actually closes under realistic recoverability levels is open.

Honest scope

What the verified work actually covers

  • Bit-length regime: 10-bit to 28-bit factors (i.e., N up to ~56-bit). Far below RSA-realistic scales (1024-bit factors and up).
  • Sample regime: Random balanced semiprimes (where documented). Default sample sizes in the tools are in the 100-300 range.
  • Recoverability metric: Most quoted numbers (92%, 89.3%, etc.) are bit-level accuracy — the fraction of individual carry bits correctly reconstructed. This is not the same as perfect sequence recovery, which is harder and only achieved on ~40% of samples in the verified tool.
  • The signal exists; the attack does not. Recovering carries with bit-accuracy ~92% is structurally meaningful but does not constitute a factoring attack at RSA scale. The "path to factoring" claim is architectural, not demonstrated.
